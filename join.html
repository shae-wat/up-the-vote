Double the Vote!

Vote YES! on Prop H

Consolidate Elections, Save Money, Increase Voter Participation

Voter turnout is much higher in the larger national elections during even years, and it plummets during odd year elections in San Francisco when local officials are the only ones on the ballot.

Prop H would move odd-year municipal elections to presidential year election cycles — when most people in San Francisco show up to vote.

Over the last decade, voter turnout in San Francisco has averaged 43% in odd year elections and 80% in presidential cycles; with the lowest odd-year voter participation amongst communities of color, the working class, and young voters. Prop H isn’t just about increasing voter turnout but also ensuring that more voters will have a say in city elections.

Plus:

● We’ll save about $7M next year in election costs

● Many cities in CA have already switched to even-years, like Los Angeles and San Jose, and have seen higher turnout across the board.

Voter participation is a cornerstone civil rights issue and it is on us to make sure everyone's voice is heard!


The California Voter Participation Rights Act (SB 415) was signed into law in 2015 — prohibiting cities in California from holding off-cycle elections if doing so results in a significant decline in voter turnout— exactly what is happening today in San Francisco.

Consistent with the state law, 55 cities across California have already taken the step to consolidate their elections— including Los Angeles, San Jose, Millbrae, Brisbane, and Modesto.

However, a Court ruled the state Voter Participation Act doesn’t apply to Charter cities, which is why we need to do this by a Charter Amendment if we want to make this change.

It’s time to update the city Charter to align with the democratic ideals of our state and city!

People of color, the working class, and youth vote disproportionately low in odd-year elections:

● In 2020, 81% of registered Chinese voters versus 41% in 2019

● In 2020, 84% of Filipino voters versus 36% in 2019

● In 2020, 82% of Latino voters versus 32% in 2019

● In 2020, 79% of voters under 24 years-old versus 21% in 2019.

Greenlining Institute has identified election consolidation as extremely effective in increasing African American participation and representation. In fact, in a 2013 report, Greenlining Institute found “at-large and off-cycle elections reform appears to be the best tool for expanding representation of African Americans in city government.”



We should be electing our important city officials when most people vote — during presidential election years — not in odd years when voter turnout is 40% or less.
